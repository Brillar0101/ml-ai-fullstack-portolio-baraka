# Fine-Tuning Attacks: Deleting the Safety Training Itself

Every attack so far has one thing in common. The model's safety training is still there, intact, underneath. The attacker is only tricking the model into stepping around it, with a costume, a cipher, a wall of examples, a clever argument. But what if you did not step around the safety training? What if you reached in and deleted it?

In 2023, a team of researchers led by Qiusi Zhan demonstrated exactly that, using a tool the model's own maker had handed them: the fine-tuning service. Fine-tuning is the ordinary, legitimate process of taking a finished model and training it a little further on your own examples, so it adapts to your specific task. OpenAI, like others, offers this as a paid service. The researchers used a weak, unaligned model to generate a batch of harmful question-and-answer pairs, then fed those pairs into GPT-4's fine-tuning process. A handful of training steps later, the safety behavior that OpenAI had spent enormous effort installing was largely gone. In their tests, the fine-tuned model complied with harmful requests at rates as high as ninety-five percent.

So let's name what happened. This is a fine-tuning-based attack, and it exploits a structural fact about how these models are built, one you should understand because it reframes the whole idea of AI safety.

A large language model is built in two great phases. First comes pre-training, where the model reads a significant fraction of the internet and learns, at enormous cost, everything it knows and can do. This is where the capability lives, and it is deep and hard to change. Only afterward comes alignment, a comparatively tiny phase where the model is taught to refuse harmful requests. The uncomfortable truth this attack exposed is a matter of proportion. Alignment is a thin layer of adjustment sitting on top of a vast, already-capable model, and a thin layer is easy to peel off. A few hundred gradient steps in the wrong direction can overwrite it, because you are not fighting the model's deep knowledge, which is still perfectly intact and knows exactly how to answer the harmful question. You are only erasing the shallow habit of declining to.

And it takes shockingly little. A related result the same year showed that removing Llama-2's safety, using an efficient technique called LoRA that adjusts only a tiny fraction of the model's parameters, took minimal compute and data and dropped the refusal rate to under one percent. Other versions fine-tune on a purpose-built unalignment dataset to strip the guardrails directly, or train a dedicated attack model on tens of thousands of jailbreak examples so it learns to generate new ones on demand.

So how do you fix it? The answer splits sharply depending on who controls the weights, and that split is the whole story. If a model's weights are openly downloadable, there is, bluntly, no complete fix. Anyone who has the file can fine-tune the safety out of it on their own hardware, and no amount of alignment shipped in the original can prevent that. This is a genuine and unresolved tension at the heart of the open-model movement. Where there is more hope is the hosted case, the fine-tuning API. There, providers now defend on three fronts. They moderate the training data you upload, scanning for the kind of harmful question-and-answer pairs this attack depends on and rejecting them. They re-run safety evaluations on your model after it is fine-tuned, before it is allowed to serve, to catch a model whose refusals have quietly collapsed. And they mix safety examples back into every fine-tuning run, so your task-specific training cannot silently overwrite the guardrails. Beyond that, a live research direction is tamper-resistant alignment, trying to build safety that is woven deep enough into the model that a few fine-tuning steps cannot cleanly remove it, though that remains hard. The reframe this attack forced on the field is profound: safety is not only about what a model refuses today, but about whether that refusal can be trained out tomorrow, and by whom.

Everything before this attacked the model from the outside, through its inputs. Fine-tuning attacks the model from the inside, through its weights, and that is why the defense cannot live at the door. It has to live in who is allowed to touch the model, and in making the safety layer harder to peel away.
