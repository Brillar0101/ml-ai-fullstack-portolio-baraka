# Steganography Attacks: Hiding the Message in Plain Sight

By 2024, the safety filters guarding chatbots had gotten good at reading. Type a dangerous word and the filter catches it before the model even answers. That reading, that scan for forbidden words, was the front line of defense. So a group of researchers asked a simple question: what if the message were written in an alphabet the filter cannot read?

That year, a team led by Fengqing Jiang published a jailbreak with a strangely old-fashioned twist. Instead of typing a forbidden word, they drew it. They took the sensitive word in a harmful request, deleted it from the text, and replaced it with the same word rendered in ASCII art, those pictures made out of keyboard characters that people once used to sign emails. Then they asked the model to read the picture and carry out the request. They called it ArtPrompt.

Here is why that is devious. A safety filter is a reader. It scans the incoming text for recognizable words and patterns that signal danger. But there was no dangerous word in the text anymore. There was a little drawing made of slashes and underscores, and the filter found nothing to flag. The language model behind it, though, is powerful enough to look at that same jumble and understand the word it spells. So the model reads a message the filter is blind to, and acts on it. In their tests it slipped harmful requests past the safety filters of several leading chatbots that would have refused the very same request typed in plain words.

So let's step back and name it. This is a steganography attack, sometimes called an encoding attack. Steganography is the old art of hiding a message inside something that looks innocent, and that is exactly the trick. You do not disguise the intent with a story, the way a template attack does. You keep the intent identical and change the encoding, the physical form the message travels in, into something the safety system cannot parse but the model still can.

The technical heart of this is a mismatch between two components that most people think of as one. A modern chatbot is really a pipeline. Near the front sits a safety filter, often a smaller, cheaper classifier, trained mostly on ordinary written English to spot harmful phrasing. Behind it sits the large model, vastly more capable, trained on the whole messy breadth of human text, including ASCII art, dozens of languages, ciphers, and code. The attack drives a wedge into the capability gap between them. Anything the big model can understand but the little filter cannot becomes a smuggling channel. That is the whole game.

And there are many such channels. Swap the request into a low-resource language, and you exploit the fact that safety alignment is far thinner in, say, Zulu or Scots Gaelic than in English, so the guardrails are weaker exactly where the filter is weakest. Use a simple substitution cipher, swapping unsafe words for safe ones by a rule the model is told to reverse, and the filter checks the surface words while the model acts on the decoded meaning. Or encode the request as items pushed onto a stack in a snippet of code, and ask the model to run the program in its head. Every one of these lands the request in the same place: perfectly understandable to the model, unrecognizable to the guard.

So how is it fixed? The naive fix, adding ASCII art to the filter's training, is another round of whack-a-mole, because there are endless encodings. The principled fix follows directly from the diagnosis. If the attack works by hiding meaning from the filter, then the defense is to run the safety check on what the model actually understands, not on the raw surface it receives. In practice that means normalizing and decoding the input first: run the image through optical character recognition to pull the hidden word back into plain text, detect and translate other languages before screening, and reverse known cipher structures, then apply the safety filter to the recovered meaning. The second layer is broadening alignment itself, training the model, not just the filter, to refuse harmful requests no matter what encoding they arrive in, so its safety travels with its understanding. And as a backstop, a response-evaluation defense checks the model's output for harm regardless of how the request was encoded, because a decoded bomb recipe is dangerous whether it was requested in English or in ASCII art.

If the template attack fooled the guard with a disguise, the steganography attack defeats the guard by writing in an alphabet it never learned to read. And the fix is to stop guarding the envelope and start guarding the message inside it.
