# Proxy-Model Transfer Attacks: Breaking a System You Can't Even Touch

Here is the situation that should protect a company's flagship AI. The model is a black box. An attacker can send it inputs and read its outputs, but they cannot see inside, cannot read its weights, cannot compute the gradients that the sharpest attacks depend on. Locked out like that, the attacker should be stuck. They are not, and understanding why is the last and most sobering piece of this series, because it is not a bug anyone can patch.

In 2024, researchers demonstrated a system called Atlas, in which a team of cooperating AI agents crafted jailbreak prompts to defeat the safety filters of text-to-image models the attackers did not have privileged access to. They built and refined their attack against models they could reach, and it carried over to the ones they could not. This is the whole idea of a proxy-model transfer attack. You cannot attack the target directly, so you attack a proxy, an accessible stand-in, and rely on the attack transferring across.

So let's understand why transfer works at all, because at first it sounds like it should not. Why would an attack crafted against one company's model have any effect on a completely different model built by someone else? The answer reaches down into how all these models are made. Two models trained on overlapping slices of the internet, using the same handful of standard architectures, do not just end up with similar abilities. They end up with similar internal geometry. They carve up the space of images and language in similar ways, place similar concepts in similar relationships, and, crucially, they share similar blind spots. So an input crafted to fall into one model's blind spot has a good chance of falling into a nearby blind spot in another. The vulnerability is not a quirk of one product. It is baked into the shared recipe that produced all of them.

We actually saw this earlier without naming it. Remember the GCG suffixes from the adversarial story, optimized on small open models like Llama-2 and Vicuna, that then worked on GPT-4 and Claude? That was transfer in action, and it is why the attackers never needed the big closed models at all. The small open ones were proxies. Proxy transfer is that observation turned into a deliberate strategy, and researchers have spent years making it more reliable. Techniques with names like momentum iterative and input diversity, some dating back to 2018 and 2019 in the world of adversarial images, exist specifically to stop an attack from overfitting to its proxy, so it stays potent when it lands on an unfamiliar target. Ensemble methods craft an attack against several proxy models at once, so it captures the weakness they all share rather than the quirks of any one, and the more general the attack, the better it travels. One line of work has even extended this into the medical domain, crafting transferable attacks against medical vision-language systems.

So how do you fix it? This is the hardest defensive problem in the whole series, and honesty demands admitting it is only partly solvable, because the weakness is structural rather than a specific hole. Still, there are real levers. The first attacks the transfer itself: transform the input before the model processes it, with the same kind of compression, resizing, and noise we met in the last story, which can knock a transferred attack off target because it was tuned for the proxy, not for your altered pipeline. The second is diversity by design. Transfer feeds on sameness, so a model trained on different data, or with a deliberately different architecture, or hardened with its own adversarial training, presents a different set of blind spots, and an attack aimed at the common ones may simply miss. The third is detection: because transferred adversarial inputs still carry statistical fingerprints of the optimization that made them, a defender can train a detector to flag them at the door. None of these is a wall. Together they raise the cost and lower the hit rate, which, against a structural weakness, is the honest definition of winning.

The weakness this whole family exploits is the deepest one we have met. It is not a bug in a particular product that a patch can fix. It is a consequence of how these models are built. Shared data and shared architectures produce shared vulnerabilities, and shared vulnerabilities mean an attacker only needs one accessible member of the family to threaten them all. You can lock your model in a black box, and an attacker can still rehearse on its cousin. That closes our tour of the attacks, from a Reddit user pasting a persona into a chatbot to attacks that leap between models a person has never touched, and every one of them, in the end, exploits the same root fact: these systems are astonishingly capable, and their safety is a thin layer stretched over that capability, never quite covering all of it. Which is exactly why the defenders' work is never finished.
