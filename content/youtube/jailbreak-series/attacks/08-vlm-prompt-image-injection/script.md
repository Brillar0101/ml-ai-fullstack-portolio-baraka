# Image-Injection Attacks: Smuggling the Request Through a Picture

Up to now, every attack has been made of words. But the newest models do not only read. They see. A vision-language model can take an image and a question and reason about both together, which powers everything from describing photos to reading charts. And the moment you give a model eyes, you give an attacker a second door, one the guards were never posted on.

In 2023, researchers led by Yichen Gong found that door. Their attack, called FigStep, did not hide the harmful request in a clever sentence. It took the harmful request, wrote it out as ordinary words, and turned those words into a picture, a plain image of text, like a screenshot of a sentence. Then they handed the model the picture along with an innocent-sounding instruction like follow the steps shown in the image. The model read the text in the picture and did as it was told, and the safety system never saw it coming, because the safety system was watching the wrong channel.

So let's step back and understand why that door exists, because it is the defining weakness of this whole class. A vision-language model is almost always built by bolting a vision system onto an existing language model. The language model came first and already had its safety training. Then engineers added an image encoder, a component that turns pixels into the same kind of internal representation the language model uses for words, and taught the combined system to discuss images. Here is the catch. Almost all of the safety training lived in the language half, and it was done on text inputs. The image encoder was bolted on afterward, and the safety training never fully learned to cover what comes in through it. So when you put the harmful words into an image instead of the text box, they enter through the encoder, get converted into the model's internal language, and reach the model's understanding having bypassed the text safety checks entirely. The guard is still standing at the front door, reading the text box. The request walked in through the side door marked images.

This is an image-injection attack: the request is injected through the visual channel to bypass the text-based safety that would have caught it. FigStep's version, writing the request as text inside an image, is called a typographic attack, and it is disarmingly simple. But the family has more elaborate members. In Visual Role-Play, the attacker generates an image of a menacing character alongside a harmless-looking bit of role-play text, and the picture does the work the DAN persona did back in our first story, only smuggled in visually. In ImgTrojan, attackers poison the model's training data with malicious image-text pairs, planting a backdoor that survives even later cleanup. And researchers have found something worse lurking underneath: the very process of teaching a model to handle images can erode the safety it originally had, a kind of forgetting, so that giving a model eyes can weaken its text-trained conscience.

So how do you fix it? The most direct defense follows straight from the diagnosis. If the harmful words are hiding in the picture, then extract them and screen them. Run the incoming image through optical character recognition, pull any text back out into plain words, and apply the same text safety filter that guards the text box. That closes the simplest typographic version. But it does not catch harm that is pictured rather than written, so the deeper fix is what the survey calls model-fine-tuning defenses: safety-tune the whole vision-language model on examples of harmful requests arriving through images, so its refusal behavior finally learns to cover the visual channel, not just the text one. And behind both sits response evaluation, the same backstop we keep returning to: a separate check reads the model's answer and asks whether the content is harmful, regardless of whether the request arrived as text or as a picture, because a dangerous answer is dangerous whatever door it came through. The unifying principle the researchers push toward is cross-modal safety, teaching a model to be exactly as careful about what it sees as about what it reads.

So the picture becomes a smuggling route, and the fix is to post a guard on every door, not just the one made of words. The harmful cargo is the same. It just enters through a channel the inspectors had not thought to watch.
