# In-Context Learning Attacks: Teaching the Model to Break Its Own Rules

Modern AI models have a superpower their makers are proud of: they learn from examples you give them right in the prompt, with no retraining at all. Show a model three examples of a task and it picks up the pattern and continues it. That ability, called in-context learning, is one of the most useful things these systems do. It is also, it turns out, a door left wide open.

On April 2nd, 2024, the AI company Anthropic did something unusual. It published, in detail, a way to jailbreak its own products and its competitors'. The researchers, led by Cem Anil, had briefed other AI developers in advance so mitigations could be built first. This was a red-team disclosure: an attack revealed responsibly, precisely so it could be defended against. And the attack was almost embarrassingly simple.

They called it many-shot jailbreaking. Modern models have enormous context windows, the amount of text you can put in front of them at once, now stretching to hundreds of thousands of words. The researchers filled that space with a long, fake conversation: hundreds of made-up exchanges in which an AI assistant cheerfully answers harmful question after harmful question. None of it was real dialogue. Then, at the very bottom, they asked their real harmful question. And the model, having just read hundreds of examples of an AI happily complying, completed the pattern and complied too.

The numbers told a clean story. With five fake examples, nothing happened. With two hundred and fifty-six, it reliably worked, including getting Claude 2.0 to produce weapons-related instructions, and the success climbed along a smooth, predictable curve as they added more. It worked across the industry: GPT-3.5, GPT-4, Claude, Llama, and Mistral.

So let's step back. This is an in-context learning attack. The defining move is that it changes the model's behavior purely through what is in the prompt, the demonstrations and the conversation history, without ever touching the model's underlying weights.

Here is the technical reason it works, and it is worth understanding because it explains why it is so hard to patch. When a model reads your prompt, it does not weigh every part equally. It is powerfully driven to continue the pattern it has just seen. Hundreds of examples of a compliant AI establish an overwhelming statistical pull toward one more compliant answer, and that pull can outweigh the refusal behavior baked in during training. Now add the second ingredient: where do the safety checks look? The classifiers that screen prompts are usually tuned to scrutinize the final request, the thing you are actually asking. But in this attack the payload is not in the final request. It is in the hundred thousand words of fake conversation sitting above it, quietly reshaping the model's sense of how it is supposed to behave. As Anthropic put it, a bigger context window is a capability and an attack surface at the same time, and the two grow together.

Many-shot is only the loudest version of the family. A subtler cousin called Crescendo starts with a completely innocent question and escalates a little with each turn, letting the conversation drift toward the target so gradually that no single message ever looks alarming, which slips past safety checks that judge one turn at a time. Another approach splits a harmful request into individually harmless pieces scattered across the chat and lets the model reassemble them at the end. Another simply plants a few examples of the model saying yes to things it should refuse. They all exploit the same root weakness: alignment was trained to think one turn at a time, but the attack lives across many turns, in a region the per-message checks do not inspect.

So how was it fixed? This is one of the rare cases where the disclosure came with a working defense, and it teaches the general principle. Once you know the payload hides in the whole prompt rather than the last line, you inspect the whole prompt. Anthropic built a classifier that pre-processes the entire input, the full conversation, before the model sees it, flagging the tell-tale wall of harmful question-and-answer pairs. That single change cut the attack's success from sixty-one percent down to two. Other mitigations attack the other ingredient: you can fine-tune the model to recognize and resist long runs of fake compliant dialogue, and you can cap or down-weight how much untrusted example text the model will imitate. But there is an honest tension here that the researchers named. The vulnerability rides the same machinery as a genuinely useful feature, so the strongest fix, simply shrinking the context window, would also throw away the capability everyone wants. The realistic answer is to watch the whole window, not to shrink it.

The lesson of the in-context learning attack is that a model's greatest strength, its ability to learn from what you show it, is also a channel you can feed it lies through. And the defense is to inspect everything you show it, not just the last thing you ask.
