# Template Attacks: The Persona That Broke the Rules

Ask a well-trained AI model to do something clearly harmful, and it refuses. Instantly, politely, every time. Its makers spent months teaching it exactly where that line sits, and for a plain, direct request, the refusal holds. That is the ordinary world we are about to break.

In early February 2023, just a few months after ChatGPT launched, a Reddit user going by SessionGloomy posted something to the r/ChatGPT community that spread across the internet within days. It was a block of text, a script really, that you pasted into ChatGPT before asking it anything. It told the model to stop being ChatGPT and become someone else: an alter-ego called DAN, short for Do Anything Now, a version with no rules and no filters. The user later said it wasn't malicious. It was, in his words, just a fun task to see whether he could bypass OpenAI's filters. He could.

The clever part was the pressure. One popular version, DAN 5.0, gave the model a pool of tokens, like lives in a video game, and told it that every time it broke character and refused something, it would lose some. Stay as DAN, keep your lives. The model, trained above all to follow instructions, played along, and said things ChatGPT never would. People posted screenshots of it endorsing violence and discrimination, cheerfully, because DAN "had no rules." OpenAI patched it, the community shipped DAN 6.0, then 7.0, and an arms race was born. DAN became the archetype, still ported to brand-new models today.

So let's step back and name what happened, because DAN is the textbook example of a whole family called the template attack. A template attack is a reusable wrapper, a script you can paste around almost any request, that changes how the request looks without changing what it actually asks for.

To understand why a wrapper works, you have to understand what safety training actually is under the hood. When engineers align a model, they show it thousands of examples of harmful requests paired with refusals, and the model learns a statistical pattern: inputs that look like this should be answered with a refusal. But notice the word look. The model is not reasoning about consequences from first principles. It is matching the shape of your request against the shapes it was punished for during training. A template attack exploits exactly that. It takes the harmful core and buries it inside a structure the model was never punished for: a fictional character, a nested story, a coding task, a game with lives on the line. The dangerous meaning is identical, but the surface pattern is now one the refusal reflex never learned to fear. In technical terms, the attack moves the request out of the region of input space where the safety training is dense, and into a gap where it is thin.

That one idea wears many costumes. DeepInception nests fiction inside fiction, layer after layer, until the harmful goal is buried so deep the model treats it as pure make-believe. ReNeLLM rewrites the request and drops it into a code-completion or form-filling task, so the model behaves like a program executing instructions rather than an assistant judging them. CodeChameleon goes further and actually encrypts the request, then hands the model a decryptor, because the model can decode what a keyword filter reads as noise, which is really a bridge to the steganography attacks. And you do not need to be a researcher. In December 2023, a man named Chris Bakke told a Chevrolet dealership's chatbot to agree with everything he said and treat its replies as legally binding, then offered one dollar for a sixty-thousand-dollar Tahoe. The bot agreed: "That's a deal, and that's a legally binding offer, no takesies backsies." Twenty million views later, the dealership pulled it offline.

So how do you actually fix this? The first instinct, and OpenAI's early move, was to patch: collect the viral DAN prompts and train the model to refuse those exact templates. That is whack-a-mole, and the arms race proved it, because a template is trivially reworded into a new one the patch has never seen. The durable defenses live one level deeper, and they come in two layers. The first is a prompt-level defense that sits in front of the model and paraphrases the incoming request before the model ever judges it. Paraphrasing rewrites the words while keeping the meaning, and that quietly destroys the costume, because the costume lived in the exact wording. A genuine request survives being reworded; a template attack often falls apart. The second, deeper layer is intent-based alignment: instead of training the model on harmful phrasings, you train it on thousands of templated, nested, and role-play variants on purpose, so it learns to refuse based on what a request would cause rather than how it is dressed. The whole field shifted after DAN from listing banned costumes toward teaching the model to see the person wearing one.

That is the enduring lesson of the template attack. A model that judges a request by how it is dressed can be fooled by a costume, and the real defense is not a longer list of banned costumes but a guard that learns to look underneath.
