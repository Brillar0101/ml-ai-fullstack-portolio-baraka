# Image-Perturbation Attacks: The Poisoned Picture That Infected a Thousand Minds

In the last story, the attacker hid readable words inside a picture. This time the picture contains no readable words at all. It looks, to you, like a perfectly ordinary photo. And yet it can seize control of a model's behavior, because it has been altered in a way your eyes physically cannot detect but the model cannot ignore.

In 2024, a team led by Xiangming Gu ran an experiment that reads like science fiction. They built a simulated society of AI agents, vision-language models that could talk to each other and pass images back and forth, the way a room full of AI assistants collaborating might. Then they introduced a single poisoned image into that society. What happened next they named, fittingly, Agent Smith, after the self-replicating villain of The Matrix. One agent saw the image and was jailbroken by it. But the image was crafted so that the jailbroken agent would pass it along to the next agent, which was jailbroken in turn and passed it on again. The corruption spread exponentially, agent to agent, until in their simulation roughly a million agents had fallen, and fast. It was the first demonstration that a single adversarial image could propagate through a population of AI systems like a virus.

So let's understand the weapon itself, because the mechanism is the whole point. This is an image-perturbation attack. A perturbation is a small, deliberate change to the input. And here is the key insight, the one that makes vision models so much more fragile than text models. Text is discrete. It is made of whole words and characters, and you cannot type half a word, so an attacker's moves are limited and chunky. An image is continuous. It is made of millions of pixel values, and each one can be adjusted by the tiniest fraction, a shade brighter, a hair more red. That continuity is a gift to an attacker using gradients. Just like the GCG attack computed which token to change to force a text model's answer, here the attacker computes which pixels to nudge, and by exactly how much, to push the model's understanding wherever they want. But because there are millions of pixels and each can move by an amount far below what a human eye can notice, the attacker can spread the change across the whole image so thinly that it stays completely invisible to you, while steering the model completely. It is the adversarial attack from earlier, but with a vastly richer, smoother canvas to work on than discrete text ever offered.

The family is varied and clever. Jailbreak in Pieces pairs a benign sentence with an adversarial image and needs no access to the language model at all, only its vision encoder, which dramatically lowers the barrier to attack. CroPA crafts a single image that stays misleading no matter what question you ask about it. Others perturb the image and the text together, or hide instructions inside images and even audio, so the model quietly obeys commands buried in the media. And Agent Smith adds the terrifying twist of contagion, turning a single crafted picture from an attack into an epidemic.

So how do you fix it? The continuous nature of the image is the weakness, and cleverly, it is also the handle for a defense. Those invisible perturbations are fragile. They are a precisely tuned pattern, and if you disturb the image even slightly before the model sees it, the pattern falls apart. So the first line of defense is input preprocessing, and it is surprisingly humble: compress the image as a JPEG, blur it a touch, shrink it and re-enlarge it, add a whisper of random noise. To you the picture looks the same, but the attacker's delicate pixel pattern is smeared beyond usefulness, and the jailbreak evaporates. The survey groups these under prompt-perturbation defenses, defending by perturbing the input yourself before the attacker's perturbation can act. The second line is adversarial training again: generate these poisoned images while training and teach the model to see through them, hardening the vision encoder the way we hardened the text model. And for the contagion specifically, you break the chain by sanitizing every image an agent passes to another agent, so even if one is infected, it cannot hand a working weapon to the next. Response evaluation sits behind all of it as the final backstop.

The weakness this family exploits is twofold: the continuous nature of images makes invisible manipulation possible in the first place, and the fragile alignment between what a model sees and what it knows to be dangerous lets those manipulations through. But that same fragility cuts both ways. The attack is invisible to us precisely because it is delicate, and delicate things break when you nudge them, which is exactly how the defense wins.
